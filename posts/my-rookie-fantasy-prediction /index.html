The Kansas City Chiefs defeated the Philadelphia Eagles in the Super Bowl this year. It has been a busy couple of months for the city of Kansas. The reason is, Kansas City also hosted the National Football League (NFL) Draft just a couple of weeks ago, from April 27 to April 29, 2023. This is the first time, the Super Bowl-winning team and the NFL Draft host city are the same, that is, the Chiefs winning the Super Bowl and Kansas City hosting the NFL Draft.

Every year, the NFL Draft is a major event and all the football fans get excited to see which player their team is going to pick. This also creates enormous chatter between college football fans, to see if any player from their school is going to get drafted.

Football season is still couple of months out, I decided to make a model similar to my NCAA model, but this time I was not worried about wins and losses, I used machine learning to predict numbers and more importantly the number of fantasy points that a rookie wide receiver would gain in this upcoming NFL season. Fantasy sports is something a lot of Americans play and it can be considered as a hobby or something to pass time. About 40 million people play fantasy football in the United States alone, (ESPN, 2021). That is a gigantic number to say the least. I personally do not play fantasy football or any other fantasy sport, but I do get it, people love it and the number of players backs it up.

To predict this number, I used an algorithm called XGBoost. I basically, looked at all the players in the NFL Draft, and singled-out wide receivers who got drafted this year. I got my data from Pro Football Reference. I have to give credit where credit is due, my instructor really cleaned up the data and gave it to me and I was able to use it for my analysis.

So, the way I did this was, I used different metrics. Just a reminder, these players have not played in the NFL yet, hence they are rookies. So how would I be able to look at data? simple really, like most of the NFL front offices who are looking to draft these players, they look at their college careers and that is quite important. College is the path you take as an athlete hoping to play in the professionals. Going back to the metrics I looked at, the college team they played for, the overall draft pick they went (eg. round one, first overall etc), now coming to some more familiar stats, total number of yards, total number of receptions, total number of touchdowns, and the total yards per reception (YPR).

Per usual, I will include my code, if you are a coding geek like me and want a better understanding of how I came to my results.

Code 
library(tidyverse)
library(tidymodels)
library(bonsai)
library(gt)

set.seed(1234)

wrstats <- read_csv("https://mattwaite.github.io/sportsdatafiles/wrdraftedstatswithratingsfixed.csv")

wrselected <- wrstats  %>%
  select(
    name,
    year,
    college_team,
    nfl_team,
    overall,
    total_yards,
    total_touchdowns,
    total_receptions,
    total_ypr,
    FantPt
  ) %>% na.omit()

player_split <- initial_split(wrselected, prop = .8)

player_train <- training(player_split)
player_test <- testing(player_split)

player_recipe <- 
  recipe(FantPt ~ ., data = player_train) %>%
  update_role(name, year, college_team, nfl_team, new_role = "ID")

xg_mod <- boost_tree(
  trees = tune(), 
  learn_rate = tune(),
  tree_depth = tune(), 
  min_n = tune(),
  loss_reduction = tune(), 
  sample_size = tune(), 
  mtry = tune(), 
  ) %>% 
  set_mode("regression") %>% 
  set_engine("xgboost")

lightgbm_mod <- 
  boost_tree() %>%
  set_engine("lightgbm") %>%
  set_mode(mode = "regression")

xg_workflow <- 
  workflow() %>% 
  add_model(xg_mod) %>% 
  add_recipe(player_recipe)

lightgbm_workflow <- 
  workflow() %>% 
  add_model(lightgbm_mod) %>% 
  add_recipe(player_recipe)

xgb_grid <- grid_latin_hypercube(
  trees(),
  tree_depth(),
  min_n(),
  loss_reduction(),
  sample_size = sample_prop(),
  finalize(mtry(), player_train),
  learn_rate()
)

player_folds <- vfold_cv(player_train)

xgb_res <- tune_grid(
  xg_workflow,
  resamples = player_folds,
  grid = xgb_grid,
  control = control_grid(save_pred = TRUE)
)

best_rmse <- select_best(xgb_res, "rmse")

final_xgb <- finalize_workflow(
  xg_workflow,
  best_rmse
)

xg_fit <- 
  final_xgb %>% 
  fit(data = player_train)

lightgbm_fit <- 
  lightgbm_workflow %>% 
  fit(data = player_train)

currentwrstats <- read_csv("https://mattwaite.github.io/sportsdatafiles/currentwrstats.csv")

wrcurrentselected <- currentwrstats  %>%
  select(
    name,
    year,
    college_team,
    nfl_team,
    overall,
    total_yards,
    total_touchdowns,
    total_receptions,
    total_ypr,
  ) %>% na.omit()

currentxgpredict <- 
  xg_fit %>% 
  predict(new_data = wrcurrentselected) %>%
  bind_cols(wrcurrentselected) 

currentxgpredict %>% 
  top_n(5, wt=.pred) %>%
  select(name, nfl_team, college_team, overall, .pred) %>%
  arrange(desc(.pred)) %>% 
  gt() %>% 
  cols_label(
    name = "Player",
    nfl_team = "Team",
    college_team = "College",
    overall = "Draft Pick",
    .pred = "Fantasy Points"
    
  ) %>%
  tab_header(
    title = "Top five wide receivers from the 2023 draft class and their predicted fantasy points",
    subtitle = "Twenty first pick, Quentin Johnston tops the list with an average of 80 points."
  ) %>%  
  tab_source_note(
    source_note = md("**By:** Samvruth Raj |  **Source:** [Pro Football Reference](https://www.pro-football-reference.com/#site_menu_link)")
  ) %>% 
  tab_style(
    style = cell_text(color = "black", weight = "bold", align = "left"),
    locations = cells_title("title")
  ) %>% 
  tab_style(
    style = cell_text(color = "black", align = "left"),
    locations = cells_title("subtitle")
  ) %>%
  tab_style(
     locations = cells_column_labels(columns = everything()),
     style = list(
       cell_borders(sides = "bottom", weight = px(3)),
       cell_text(weight = "bold", size=12)
     )
   ) %>%
  opt_row_striping() %>% 
  opt_table_lines("none")

As you can see from the table above, these are the top-five rookie wide receivers and how many fantasy points they would earn if you drafted them into your fantasy football team. Just a reminder, this is not by any shape or form going to happen but chances are it will, which is why it is called a prediction.

Let us look at the top-five rookie wide receivers on how they compare to some of the players from the 2022 NFL season,

Quentin Johnston - WR, Los Angeles Chargers

As per my model and my prediction, Quentin Johnston is number one on my list and is predicted to get 80 fantasy points in the upcoming NFL season. The four-star prospect played three years for the TCU Horned Frogs before committing for the NFL Draft. Johnston helped TCU pull one of the biggest upsets of the season defeating Michigan in the College Football Playoff Semi-final. In that game, Johnston had six receptions for 163 yards and one touchdown. For his commanding performance, Johnston was named the College Football Playoff Semifinal Offensive Most Valuable Player. Johnston was selected by the Los Angeles Chargers as their 21st pick in round one of the NFL Draft. My prediction would compare Quentin Johnston to Marquez Valdes-Scantling who is a wide receiver for the reigning Super Bowl champs, Kansas City Chiefs who had 80 points in the 2022 NFL season.

Zay Flowers - WR, Baltimore Ravens

Zay Flowers takes the second spot on my list, and as per my model is predicted to get 73.5 fantasy points in his rookie season. A three-star recruit coming out of high school, the Florida native, attended Boston College from 2019-2022 and committed for the NFL Draft in December, 2022. Flowers became the second wide receiver in Boston College history to be named first team All-Atlantic Coast Conference after he finished his sophomore season with 56 receptions for 892 yards and nine touchdowns. Zay Flowers was selected right after Quentin Johnston by the Baltimore Ravens as their 22nd pick in round one of the NFL Draft. My prediction would compare Zay Flowers to Russell Gage from the Tampa Bay Buccaneers with 72 points, or Rashid Shaheed of the New Orleans Saints who had 72.5 points in the 2022 NFL season.

Jordan Addison - WR, Minnesota Vikings

Jordan Addison is the third wide receiver on my list. As per my model, Addison is predicted to get 73 fantasy points as well in his rookie season. A four-star recruit who split time playing football for the University of Pittsburgh and the University of Southern California (USC). At his time at USC, Addison had a First-Team All-Pac-12 selection in 2022. The former USC Trojan was picked right after Zay Flowers as the 23rd pick for the Minnesota Vikings in round one of the NFL Draft. My Prediction would again compare Addison to Russell Gage from Tampa Bay and Rashid Shaheed of the Saints, both of them having 72 and 72.5 points each.

Jaxon Smith-Njigba - WR, Seattle Seahawks

Jaxon Smith-Njigba is fourth on my list and as per my model is predicted to get 71 fantasy points in his first season with the Seahawks. A five-star recruit coming out of high school, Smith-Njigba committed to play college football for the Ohio State Buckeyes. In the 2021 season, Smith-Njigba set school records for most catches in a single game with 15, which he did on two separate occasions, he also had the most receiving yards in a single season with 1,606 yards. Smith-Njigba was limited to three games in the 2022 season due to a hamstring injury, and withdrew from his senior year of college eligibility and committed for the NFL Draft. Jaxon Smith-Njigba was selected by the Seattle Seahawks as their 20th pick in round one of the NFL Draft. My prediction would compare Smith-Njigba to Marvin Jones of the Detroit Lions who had 70.9 points in the 2022 NFL season.

Jayden Reed - WR, Green Bay Packers

Last but not the least, Jayden Reed is the fifth wide receiver and as per my model, Reed is predicted to get 68 fantasy points in his rookie season. A three-star recruit, the Chicago native split his college career between Western Michigan and the Michigan State Spartans. At his time in Michigan State, Reed had two, second-team All-Big Ten selections (2021 ,2022). Jayden Reed won the 2021 Peach Bowl Most Valuable Player, after Michigan State beat Pittsburgh. Reed was selected by the Green Bay Packers as their 50th pick in round two of the NFL Draft. My prediction would compare Jayden Reed to DJ Chark of the Carolina Panthers who ended the 2022 season with 68 points. The real question, I would be asking is who is going to be throwing the ball to Jayden Reed? for him to get 68 points this upcoming season, something I certainly cannot predict using any model, it is up to the Green Bay front office to sort that one out.
